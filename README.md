## Project Overview

This project explores the global decline in fertility rates and the key factors driving that shift — including education levels, economic development, life expectancy, and female population ratios. Using Tableau, I built a series of interactive visualizations that tell a data-driven story about how the world's demographics are changing and what that means for governments, healthcare systems, and workforce planning.

The project analyzes country-level data across multiple indicators to answer one central question: why are people having fewer children, and what happens next?

---

## Key Findings

- Richer nations (U.S., Germany, Japan) have low fertility rates, creating aging workforce challenges
- Developing nations (Nigeria, Afghanistan) have high fertility, straining education and resource systems
- As the Human Development Index rises, fertility rates consistently fall across all regions
- Countries where women average more years of education show significantly lower birth rates
- High-fertility countries tend to have lower female population ratios, pointing to disparities in maternal health and gender equality

## Why This Project Matters

Falling fertility rates affect every country differently. For developed nations, the challenge is sustaining a workforce with fewer young people entering it. For developing nations, the challenge is managing rapid population growth without enough infrastructure to support it. This project uses data to show both sides of that story and highlights how investing in education — especially for women — is one of the most effective levers governments have.
